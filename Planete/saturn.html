Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest planet in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant with an average radius about nine times that of Earth. Although its density is only one-eighth that of the average Earth, due to its large volume, Saturn's mass is 95 times that of Earth. The name derives from the eponymous god of Roman mythology, the Titan counterpart Cronus, from Greek mythology ;his astronomical symbol (♄) is a stylized representation of the sickle of the god of agriculture. Saturn's interior is most likely composed of an iron-nickel core and rock (silicon and oxygen compounds). The core is surrounded by a layer of metallic hydrogen, an intermediate layer of liquid hydrogen and liquid helium, and finally a gaseous outer layer. Saturn has a pale yellow hue due to ammonia crystals in its upper atmosphere. An electric current in the metallic hydrogen layer is thought to give rise to Saturn's planetary magnetic field, which is weaker than Earth's but has a magnetic moment 580 times that of Earth due to Saturn's larger size. Saturn's magnetic field strength is about one-twentieth that of Jupiter. The atmosphere outside is generally calm although prolonged thunderstorm patterns may persist. On Saturn, winds of about 1,800 km/h blow, stronger than on Jupiter but not as high as those on Neptune. The planet's most famous feature is its prominent ring system, which is composed mostly of ice particles with a smaller amount of rocky debris and dust. The discovery of 83 satellites orbiting the planet has been confirmed, of which 53 have official names. Titan, the largest moon of Saturn and the second largest in the Solar System, is larger than the planet Mercury, although less massive, and is the only satellite in the Solar System that has a substantial atmosphere. Seen from Earth, Saturn appears to be a bright star in the sky, easily visible to the naked eye. It was only after the invention of the telescope, however, that its rings and satellites were discovered. Although the quality of observational instruments has evolved, the sending of space probes has revealed unprecedented detail. Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, and Voyager 2 passed close to Saturn, but the complexity of their findings motivated the sending of an orbiter, Cassini, which sent the Huygens probe to Titan's surface.

Internal structure

Standard planetary models suggest that Saturn's interior is similar to Jupiter's, with a small rocky core surrounded by hydrogen and helium, with traces of various volatiles. The temperature, pressure, and density inside the planet steadily increase as it moves toward the core, causing hydrogen to be a metal in the deeper layers. The core has a similar composition to Earth's, but is denser. In 2004, scientists estimated that the core must be 9-22 times the mass of Earth, which corresponds to a diameter of about 25,000 km. In the core, temperatures of nearly 12,000 °C and a pressure of 10 million atmospheres are reached. The core is surrounded by a thick layer of liquid metallic hydrogen, followed by a liquid layer of molecular hydrogen and helium that turns to gas as altitude increases. The outermost layer stretches for 1,000 km and consists of a gaseous atmosphere. Saturn , like Jupiter, radiates 2.5 times more energy into space than it receives from the Sun. Only part of this energy can be attributed to the Kelvin-Helmholtz mechanism ; an additional mechanism that would explain the heat generated is that of a "rain" of helium droplets deep inside Saturn. As the droplets descend through the lower-density hydrogen, the process releases heat through friction and leaves Saturn's outer layers helium-poor.

Atmosphere

Saturn's outer atmosphere contains 96.3% molecular hydrogen and 3.25% helium. The proportion of helium is significantly deficient compared to the abundance of this element in the Sun. The amount of elements heavier than helium ( metallicity ) is not precisely known, but the proportions are assumed to correspond to the primordial abundances since the formation of the Solar System . The total mass of these heavier elements is estimated to be 19-31 times the mass of Earth, with a significant fraction located in the planetary core region. Traces of ammonia, acetylene, ethane, propane, phosphine, and methane have been detected in Saturn's atmosphere. The upper clouds are composed of ammonia crystals, which give it its typical yellowish appearance, while the lower level clouds appear to be composed of ammonium hydrosulfide (NH 4 SH ) or water. Ultraviolet radiation from the Sun causes the photolysis of methane in the upper atmosphere, leading to a series of chemical reactions of the hydrocarbons, the resulting products being carried down by atmospheric eddies. This photochemical cycle is modulated by Saturn's annual seasonal cycle.
