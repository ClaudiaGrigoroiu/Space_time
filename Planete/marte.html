Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and the penultimate largest planet in the Solar System. Mars is named after the Roman god of war and is often referred to as the Red Planet, because the predominant iron oxide on its surface gives it a distinctive reddish appearance among astronomical bodies visible to the naked eye. Mars is a telluric planet with a thin atmosphere, having surface features reminiscent of both the impact craters of the Moon and Earth-like valleys, deserts and polar ice caps. The days and seasons are comparable to Earth's because the rotation period as well as the axial tilt relative to the ecliptic plane are very similar. Mars is home to Mount Olympus, the largest volcano and most famous mountain of any planet in the Solar System, and Valles Marineris, one of the largest canyons in the Solar System. The Northern Hemisphere basin covers 40% of the planet and can be a sign of huge impact. Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are small and irregularly shaped. They may be asteroidscaptured, similar to 5261 Eureka, a Mars Trojan. Mars has been explored by numerous unmanned spacecraft. Mariner 4, launched by NASA on November 28, 1964, was the first spacecraft to visit Mars, with the closest approach to the planet occurring on July 15, 1965. Mariner 4 detected the faint Martian radiation belt, measured at about 0.1 % of that of Earth and captured the first images of another planet from deep space. On July 20, 1976, Viking 1 made the first successful landing on the Martian surface. Although the Soviet spacecraft Mars 3had a soft start in Dec 1971, lost contact after a few seconds. [26] On July 4, 1997, the Mars Pathfinder spacecraft landed on Mars, and on July 5, the mission's rover, Sojourner , became the first robotic rover to operate on Mars. Pathfinder was followed by the Spirit and Opportunity rovers, which landed in January 2004 and operated until March 22, 2010 and June 10, 2018, respectively. The Mars Express Orbiter, the first European Space Agency spacecraft to reached orbit on 25 December 2003. NASA landed the Curiosity rover on August 6, 2012, as part of its Mars Science Laboratory mission to investigate Martian climate and geology. On 24 September 2014, the Indian Space Research Organization became the fourth space agency to visit Mars when its interplanetary Mars Orbiter Mission successfully entered orbit. The United Arab Emirates became the fifth country to successfully undertake a mission to Mars, inserting an orbiter into the Martian atmosphere on 9 February 2021 and China the sixth country when the Tianwen mission -1 entered the planet's orbit on February 10, 2021. NASA's Perseverance rover successfully landed on Mars on February 18, 2021.

Atmosphere

Mars lost its magnetosphere 4 billion years ago, probably due to numerous asteroid impacts, so the solar wind interacts directly with the Martian ionosphere, decreasing the atmospheric density by removing atoms from the outer layer. Both Mars Global Surveyor and Mars Express have detected ionized atmospheric particles escaping into space in the wake of Mars, and this atmospheric loss is being studied by the MAVEN orbiter. Compared to Earth, Mars' atmosphere is very thin. Atmospheric pressure varies from a low of 30 Pa on Mount Olympus to over 1,155 Pa in Hellas Planitia, with an average surface-level pressure of 600 Pa. On Earth, such pressure is found at an altitude of 35 km above sea level. The average surface pressure is only 0.6% of Earth's. The pressure in the Martian atmosphere decreases with altitude more slowly than on Earth, the relative decrease occurring approximately every 10.8 km (on Earth approximately every 6 km). This is due to the much lower gravitational acceleration on the surface of Mars (about 38% of that of Earth). The atmosphere on Mars consists of approximately 96% carbon dioxide, 1.93% argon and 1.89% nitrogen along with traces of oxygen and water. The atmosphere is quite thin, containing particles about 1.5 Âµm in diameter, which give the Martian sky an opaque color when seen from the surface. It can take on a pink hue due to iron oxide particles suspended in the atmosphere.

Orbit and rotation

The average distance of Mars from the Sun is about 230 million km, and the orbital period (Martian year) lasts as long as 687 days on Earth. A day on Mars (called sol) is only slightly longer than a day on Earth: 24 hours, 39 minutes, and 35.244 seconds. A Martian year is equal to 1.8809 Earth years or 1 year, 320 days and 18.2 hours. The axial tilt of Mars is 25.19 degrees relative to its orbital plane, which is similar to the axial tilt of the Earth. As a result, Mars has seasons like Earth, although they are almost twice as long on Mars because its orbital period is much longer. In its current orientation, the north pole of Mars points in a direction similar to the location of the star Deneb. Mars has a relatively pronounced orbital eccentricity of about 0.09; among the planets of the Solar System only Mercury has a greater orbital eccentricity. Mars is known to have had a much more circular orbit in the past. At one point, 1.35 million years ago (Earth years), Mars had an eccentricity of about 0.002, much less than Earth's today. Mars' eccentricity cycle is 96,000 Earth years, compared to Earth's cycle of 100,000 years. Mars has a much longer eccentricity cycle with a period of 2.2 million Earth years, and this overlaps the 96,000 year cycle in the eccentricity graphs. Over the past 35,000 years, Mars' orbit has become somewhat more eccentric due to the gravitational effects of the other planets. The closest distance between Earth and Mars will continue to decrease slightly for the next 25,000 years.
